# Idea Brief: NYC Public Software Factory

**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Status:** Shaped → Planning

## Problem

NYC's 35,000+ nonprofits and 130+ city agencies are drowning in terrible or nonexistent software. 64% of nonprofits lack in-house technical staff. Only 3% of their budgets go to technology. The city spent $100M on MyCity and got a link aggregator. Government software contracting is a broken, bloated market — traditional vendors charge millions for mediocre results. Meanwhile, the communities these organizations serve — tenants facing eviction (119K filings/year), food-insecure families (1.2M people), immigrants navigating 200+ languages — bear the cost of this technology failure every day.

The market has no incentive to fix this. The people who need the software can't pay for it. The organizations that serve them can barely keep the lights on.

## Constraints

- FileScience's AI software factory is proprietary IP — the factory itself is not open-sourced
- Must be a separate entity from FileScience (PBC structure)
- NYC-based, launching in 6–12 months
- Must be self-sustaining — not dependent on perpetual philanthropy
- Aligned with Mayor Mamdani's administration priorities (public good tech, tenant protection, language access, digital sanctuary)
- Must solve the maintenance problem — no build-and-abandon

## Options Considered

### Option A: Domain-Focused Forge (Deep)
Pick 1–2 domains (housing/tenant defense, food network coordination). Go deep. Build production tools, partner with existing orgs like JustFix and City Harvest.
- Gains: Deep impact, easier to prove the model, referenceable case studies
- Costs: Slower to diversify, narrower narrative
- Complexity: Medium

### Option B: NYC Nonprofit Tech Partner + Public Sector Rebuilder (Broad)
Position as the free/ultra-low-cost tech partner for NYC nonprofits AND city/state/county agencies. Take on projects from orgs that need tools. Rebuild existing government software (websites, portals, benefits systems) at a fraction of traditional contractor costs. Community work is free; public sector contracts are cheap. Government revenue cross-subsidizes community work.
- Gains: Broad impact, sustainable revenue model, massive demand pipeline (130+ city agencies, state offices, MTA, NYCHA, CUNY, public hospitals, libraries). Aligns with Mamdani CTO Lisa Gelobter's mandate. Narrative: "public option for software"
- Costs: Scope creep risk, operational complexity of running a consultancy, maintenance burden across many tools
- Complexity: High operationally

### Option C: Language-First (Babel NYC)
Focus entirely on the civic language access gap. NYC has 200+ languages, strong Local Law 30 mandate, catastrophically poor compliance. Build multilingual civic communication infrastructure.
- Gains: Highest AI leverage, addresses gap that literally killed people (Hurricane Ida), reusable platform
- Costs: Low-resource languages are hard, requires community trust-building
- Complexity: Medium

### Option D: Neighborhood Operating System (Hyperlocal)
Pick one NYC neighborhood (e.g., Mott Haven, South Bronx) and build an integrated platform covering food, housing, benefits, mutual aid, and community coordination.
- Gains: Highest per-person impact, visible transformation
- Costs: Highest complexity, many simultaneous partner relationships
- Complexity: High

## Chosen Approach

**Option B: NYC Nonprofit Tech Partner + Public Sector Rebuilder** — the dual-track model where community tools are free and public-sector contracts are ultra-low-cost. The AI factory compresses production costs so dramatically that government contracts at 5–10% of traditional pricing are still margin-positive, funding the free community work.

**Why:** It's the only option that's self-sustaining without perpetual philanthropy. It creates the broadest impact pipeline. It aligns perfectly with the Mamdani administration's priorities and the "public option for software" framing. And it leverages a political relationship with Mayor Mamdani's office and the democratic socialist governing philosophy of decommodification.

- 78% of NYC nonprofits receive government funding; average payment delay is 6.5 months
- Eviction filings surged 440% post-moratorium; Right to Counsel representation dropped from 71% to 42%
- 107 NYC neighborhoods have zero food distribution points
- Hurricane Ida: English-only emergency alerts contributed to 13 preventable deaths
- Small business tech adoption is under 10% in Washington Heights and Southern Boulevard (Bronx)
- MyCity portal: $100M spent, functions as a link aggregator

- Digital Sanctuary City executive order (Feb 2026)

### AI factory economics
- Token costs fell 99.7% in 26 months
- Sustainable factory run rate: $1–2M/year (5–10 engineers + API + infra)
- By 2028: estimated 5–10x reduction in human labor per unit of software
- What traditional contractors charge $5M for, an AI factory can deliver for $50K–$200K

### Tech philanthropy lessons (from research)
- DataKind's 2025 pivot: volunteer models produce episodic value, not sustained capability
- Bahmni/OpenMRS coalition model is the gold standard for maintained open-source
- The "last mile" maintenance problem kills most donated software — AI factory compresses maintenance costs too
- PBC structure (Nava PBC model) balances mission-lock with competitive salaries and investment capacity
